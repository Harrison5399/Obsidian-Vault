## Viete
- lived (1530-1603)
	- French
- First to use variables to describe constants/parameters
- He did not have many students (did the others?)
- his algebra was often tied to geometry, not so much as purely arithmetic or abstract
	- strong links to geometry
	- he didnt trust the algebra on its own without testing it with some geometry
	- posibly grounded it in geometry so that it was philosophically accepted
	- things were applicable to geometry, 
- a lot of his work was based appon the analytic methods of the greek, (funamentally geometric), but he expanded apon it, and saw algebra as a tool to solve geometric problems more efficiently
	- homogeneity? and geometric verification were non-negotiable to him
- he was the first person who introduced notations for the problem (introducing parameters not just the unknowns) and operations were able to act on the letters and the results can just be obtained at the end of the calculation by a simple replacement

## Al-Khwarizmi
- Lived (c. 780 - c. 850)
	- During the Islamic Golden Age
- Around 820 he worked at the House of Wisdom in Baghdad, the grand library in Bagdad, the intellectual capital during the Islamic golden age
	- around 820 appointed astronomer and head of the library
- Influenced the work of later Islamic and European scholars
- Al-Jabr (compiled around 820) (The compendius book on calculation by completion and balancing)
	- first systematic solution of linear and quadratic equations
		- demonstrated how to complete the square
			- provided geometrical justifications 
		- First person to treat algebra as an independent discipline
			- introduced the methods of "reduction" and "balancing"
		- the english term algebra comes from the name of this book
		- covered a large range of problems in trade, and inheritance
		- gave way of seeing modern mathematical terms as "algebra objects"
- Algorithmo de Numero Indorum (textbook on Indian arithmetic)
	- created a standard for indian numerals, and introduced the decimal-based positional number system to the western world
- In the 12th century, his works were spread to europe through latin translations, making an impact on the advance of mathematics in europe
- more "elementry" than what diophantus problems were
- he likely didnt know the works of diophantus
