## Alexandria

- They used flour to plan the layout of the city, they didn't have chalk, hungry birds ate up all the flour, this was taken as a sign that the new city would grow to feed the world

- Pollard and Reid argue that Alexandria was very influential
- Known for two things, its great library and its great lighthouse
	- The lighthouse was known as one of the seven wonders of the ancient world
#### Two methods of proof
- Proof by reductio ad absurdum
	- Proof by contradiction
	- First by Hippocrates of Chios
	- You want to prove that S is true, assume the opposite of S is true, show that this assumption leads to some contradiction (absurdity), because not-S leads to a contradiction, conclude S must be true
- Proof by exhaustion
	- First by Eudoxus of Cnidus
	- Brute force, double contradiction
	- Break proposition down into cases, prove a contradiction for each of these cases

## Eudoxus of Cindus

#### Impossible Problems
- Square the circle (quadrature of the circle), construct a square with the same area as a given circle
- Rectify the circle, construct a line segment the same length as the circumference of a given circle
- Double the cube (the Delian problem), given any line segment AB, construct another line segment CD, so that a cube with side length CD has exactly twice the volume of a cube with side length AB
- Trisect the angle, given any angle ABC, construct point D so that mABD = 1/3 mABC

#### Elements
- Deductive treatment of all the "known" mathematics at the time, in 13 books, dates around 300BCE. Synthesis, rather than analysis.
- It was not wholly original by Euclid, even the title was borrown from Hippocrates of Chios's version
- Books
	- Book 1, triangle geometry, borrowed heavily from the Pythagoreans
	- Book 5,proportion, from Eudoxus
	- Book 10, irrational numbers, from Theaetetus, and Pythagoreans
	- Book 7 & 8, number theory, unknown origins
		- First person that we know of who proved that there were infinite number of primes
			- Proof on slides
		- Defined perfect numbers, a number is perfect if it is equal to the sum of its proper factors
			- Smallest perfect number is 6, $6=1+2+3$
			- Second is 28, $28=1+2+14+4+7$
			- There is a pattern,
				- Relates perfect numbers to Mersenne primes
					- An even number, n, is perfect if and only if it has the form
					- $n=2^{p-1}(2^p-1)$
					- where p is a positive integer and (2^p -1) is a prime number
					- Euclid proved the "if" and Leonhard Euler proved the "only if"
- Euclid edited some of his proofs so that they used Eudoxus' method of proportions
	- He liked Eudoxus
- The geometry textbook for 2200 years
- One of the first books printed on Gutenberg's printing press
- Other books by Euclid
	- Data, on the nature and implication of 'givens' in geometric problems
		- If i give you this set of information, what can be constructed or deduced
	- Optics on the geometry of vision
		- Unlike other scholars, Euclid considered the mathematics and not just the philosophy of vision
		- After elements, this kept its relevance the longest
	- Phenomena, 18 propositions on spherical geometry in astronomy
		- Largely ignored by scholars because later mathematicians (Apollonius, Hipparchus, Ptolemy) all surpassed it
			- Fully translated to English for the first time in 1996

## Eratosthenes of Cyrene
- Born in 276 BCE in Cyrene, Libya. Died in 194 BCE in Alexandria, Egypt
- Appointed the chief librarian at the Library of Alexandria in 235 BCE
- Calculations estimating the circumference of the Earth, the tilt of earth axis, the distance from the earth to the sun, and the distance from the earth to the moon
- He was the first to use the idea of latitude and longitude, Alexandria was his "center of the world", as compared todays 0deg Greenwich location
- Said there were good and bad in every nation, criticized Aristotle for his divisive world views of Geeks (worthy) vs barbarians (unworthy)
- Said to be the second most accomplished scholar of his age
	- Archimedes referred to his as "beta"
	- When talking about him, there is an attitude of "jack of all trades, mast of none"
- In his last year, like Euler, he became blind, the inability to read and observe nature depressed him
	- Its believe that he committed suicide by voluntary starvation
- All but 1 of his works have been lost (they were all stored in the library)
	- That work, Catasterisms, is about constellations
- Most of his works come from Pappus of Alexandria, A Greek geometer of 4th century CE
	- Pappus work Synagoge (writed 340 CE), aimed to collect all the most important work of Greek philosophers 
#### Sieve of Erathosthenes
- Algorithm to find all primes from 1-N
- List out all numbers from 1-N, start with the first (not including 1) call it $i$, cross out all numbers that are a multiple of $i$, $i$ is prime, set $i$ to the next number not crossed out and repeat
## Archimedes of Syracuse
- Lived from 287 to 212 BCE, in Syracuse on the island of Sicily (colony of Greece)
- He was from a colony, he spoke a hillbilly-accent greek
- Archimedes screw
	- Encased screw to transport water upwards,
	- Can be used as a generator or in sewage management
- The only personal stuff that we know about him, is from him, in The Sand Reckoner
	- There had been a biography about him but it is long gone
- By the end of his life he had a wide reputation, his death was depicted by many artists
- Most of his works are in the form of letters to other scholars
	- All of them worked in Alexandria
	- One of them was Eratosthenes, who was the chief librarian
	- Others were Conon and Dositheus who were heads of the mathematics school
- His surviving works are
	- Measurement of the circle
		- Estimated pi
	- On the Sphere and Cylinder (books 1 and 2)
	- On Conoids and Spheroids
	- Balancing Planes (about centers of gravity)
	- Quadrature of the Parabola
	- On Spirals
	- The Sand Reckoner
		- Large numbers, trying to calculate number of grains of sand
	- 3 books dedicated to Eratosthenes
		- Method
		- Stomachion
		- Floating Bodies
- The siege of Syracuse
	- 214 BCE (Pro-Roman) King Hieron 2 of Syracuse dies, the (pro-carthage) Republic of Syracuse is proclaimed (they flipped sides, there was the battle between carthage and rome going on at this time)
	- 213 BCE Roman general Marcus Claudius Marecllus leads an army and a fleet to besiege Syracuse
	- Archimedes came up with a few defensive weapons for Syracuse including
		- The claw of Archimedes - a crane and hook that tipped over the Roman ships, sinking them, there is evidence that this was used
		- The Archimedes heat ray - used solar rays to set the roman ships of fire, its thought that a lens to do this was not able to be made at the time
	- He was killed by a roman soldier during this
		- His last words were reportedly, "don't disturb my circles"
#### Archimedes Palimpsest
- How were things recorded?
	- Papyrus roll, cross-hatched, horizontal and vertical rods pounded down,
		- Wrapped around a wooden rod or two
		- Popular from 6th c. BCE - 4th c. CE
	- Codex, bound books, first made with papyrus pages
		- More durable pages were needed, so sometimes they used animal skins
		- Now they are made of wood pulp
	- Palimpsest, erasing the earlier writing and writing the new stuff on top of it, usually perpendicular to the earlier writing
		- The book called Archimedes Palimpsest was a 13th century Christian prayer book used by monks
- Things were transcripted from roll-to-codex in the late 1st c. CE
	- Home and Euclid were among the first transcriptions, but not Archimedes
		- "Archimedes was though too difficult to be fundamental"
		- Transcribers did not feel rushed to transfer the Method to codex because very few people unrolled the Archimedes' scrolls
		- Many works around 4th c. and 5th c. CE were transcribed from the Great Library to Constantinople
			- Eutocius of Ascalon (480 - 530) was a Palestinian Geek, living in Constantinople
				- He traveled to Alexandria to do research and then explained Archimedes and Apollonius to his students
				- He worked with Isidore of Miletus (442-537) to make a new edition of Archimedes with Eutocius' commentaries
				- Earlier works were done in all caps, stating in the 9th c. the writing was mostly done in lowercase
				- In 1204: Christian armies sack and burn Constantinople destroying the works in the city's scriptorium
					- Some of the works, including Archimedes, wind up in the pockets of invaders
						- Taken to Italy, where there were 3 copies
							- Codex C is what became the palimpsest and a prayer book in the 13th century
							- July 1907 Danish researcher, Johan Ludvig Heiberg, finds the rest of the text, he was able to read it with a magnifying glass, he made some errors in the math, not the Greek, he was a linguist, he returned it to around where it was found in Constantinople
								- Early 1920s the ottoman empire lost in WW2 and in the turmoil the palimpsest goes missing in 1922
									- For 70+ years the palimpsest is in the hands of a French business man, it was well preserved, but after his death in 1956 his daughter moved it to the cellar
										- 1998 they brought it to a famous auction house, they were sued twice by Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem, saying it was stolen and they shouldn't have it, but it was still sold
											- Billionaire bought it for 2.2 million dollars, reportedly it was Jeff Bezos
- It was then recovered where 7 works were uncovered by Archimedes, commentaries of Aristotle
